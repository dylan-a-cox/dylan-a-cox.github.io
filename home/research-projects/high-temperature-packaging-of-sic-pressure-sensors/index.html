High Temperature Packaging of SiC Pressure Sensors

Recently as part of the DARPA HOTS program, I was part of a team involved in high temperature package development for high temperature silicon and silicon carbide pressure sensors.  Developing electronic sensors that can operate at temperatures higher than molten aluminum is incredibly difficult, and a poor choice of packaging can render even the best-designed sensors completely unusable.  Materials exhibit unpredictable electrical properties, making accurate modeling difficult.  As such, package development on this project involved extensive trial and error; a full account of every failed packaging attempt would be rather gratuitous, so this is a brief summary of the highlights.  

At high temperatures, sapphire is one of the few materials that retains a sufficiently high resistance for effective packaging of high-impedance piezoelectric sensors.  It is also difficult to machine, requiring water cooling and diamond tooling.

High temperature tests were necessary to determine the effectiveness of gold wire bonds to different materials.  Here the continuity for wire bonds between silver nanoparticle ink is compared to solid gold and silver samples.

At high temperatures, different materials expand at different rates, which often leads to stress induced cracking and failure.  In general, most metals have far higher coefficients of thermal expansion than many ceramics at elevated temperatures.  However, due to the electromagnetic shielding requirements for the project, mixing metallic and ceramic components was unavoidable.  To best match the coefficients of thermal expansion in the material, the metallic components in critical locations were machined from kovar in-house.

A photo of the completed package is shown below having survived at 800C for multiple hours.  While the performance was sufficient to meet the metrics for the project, long-term longevity had many issues, and the package had to be handled with the utmost care to avoid breakage.  In the field of high temperature sensing, high temperature package development remains an important area of focus.
